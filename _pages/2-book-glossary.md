Definitions from mcgraw-hill

1.  *Absorption costing*: The accounting procedure whereby all manufacturing costs, including fixed costs, are included in the cost of goods manufactured
1.  *Accelerated method of depreciation*: A method of depreciating an asset’s cost that allocates greater amounts of depreciation to an asset’s early years of useful life
1.  *Account balance*: The difference between the amounts recorded on the two sides of an account, the **NET**.
2.  *Account form balance sheet*: A balance sheet that lists assets on the left and liabilities and owner’s equity on the righ. See also: Report form balance sheet  
3.  *Accounting*: The process by which financial information about a business is recorded, classified, summarized, interpreted, and communicated to owners, managers, and other interested parties
4.  *Accounting cycle*: A series of steps performed during each accounting period to classify, record, and summarize data for a business and to produce needed financial information
5.  *Accounting Standards Codification*: The source of authoritative U.S. GAAP
6.  *Accounting Standards Update*: Changes to Accounting Standards Codification are communicated through Accounting Standards Update covering approximately 90 topics
7.  *Accounting system*: A process designed to accumulate, classify, and summarize financial data
8.  *Accounts*: Written records of the assets, liabilities, and owner’s equity of a business
9.  *Accounts payable*: Amounts a business must pay in the future
10. *Accounts payable ledger*: A subsidiary ledger that contains a separate account for each creditor
11. *Accounts receivable*: Claims for future collection from customers
12. *Accounts receivable ledger*: A subsidiary ledger that contains credit customer accounts
13. *Accounts receivable turnover*: A measure of the speed with which sales on account are collected; the ratio of net credit sales to average receivables
14. *Accrual basis*: A system of accounting by which all revenues and expenses are matched and reported on financial statements for the applicable period, regardless of when the cash related to the transaction is received or paid
15. *Accrued expenses*: Expense items that relate to the current period but have not yet been paid and do not yet appear in the accounting records
16. *Accrued income*: Income that has been earned but not yet received and recorded
17. *Acid-test ratio*: A measure of immediate liquidity; the ratio of quick assets to current liabilities
18. *Adjusting entries*: Journal entries made to update accounts for items that were not recorded during the accounting period
19. *Adjustments*: See Adjusting entries
20. *Aging the accounts receivable*: Classifying accounts receivable balances according to how long they have been outstanding
21. *Allowance method*: A method of recording uncollectible accounts that estimates losses from uncollectible accounts and charges them to expense in the period when the sales are recorded
22. *Amortization*: The process of periodically transferring the acquisition cost of intangible assets with estimated useful lives to an expense account
23. *Appropriation of retained earnings*: A formal declaration of an intention to restrict dividends
24. *Articles of partnership*: See Partnership agreement
25. *Asset turnover*: A measure of the effective use of assets in making sales; the ratio of net sales to total assets
26. *Assets*: Property owned by a business
27. *Audit trail*: A chain of references that makes it possible to trace information, locate errors, and prevent fraud
28. *Auditing*: The review of financial statements to assess their fairness and adherence to generally accepted accounting principles
29. *Auditor’s report*: An independent accountant’s review of a firm’s financial statements
30. *Authorized capital stock*: The number of shares authorized for issue by the corporate charter
31. *Average collection period*: The ratio of 365 days to the accounts receivable turnover; also called the *number of days’ sales in receivables*
32. *Average cost method*: A method of inventory costing using the average cost of units of an item available for sale during the period to arrive at cost of the ending inventory
33. *Average method of process costing*: A method of costing that combines the cost of beginning inventory for each cost element with the costs during the current period
34. *Average number of days in inventory*: The average number of days for merchandise inventory to be sold after it is purchased
35. *Balance ledger form*: A ledger account form that shows the ­balance of the account after each entry is posted
36. *Balance sheet*: A formal report of a business’s financial condition on a certain date; reports the assets, liabilities, and owner’s equity of the business
37. *Bank draft*: A check written by a bank that orders another bank to pay the stated amount to a specific party
38. *Bank reconciliation statement*: A statement that accounts for all differences between the balance on the bank statement and the book balance of cash
39. *Banker’s year*: A 360-day period used to calculate interest on a note
40. *Bill of lading*: A business document that lists goods accepted for transportation
41. *Blank endorsement*: A signature of the payee written on the back of the check that transfers ownership of the check without specifying to whom or for what purpose
42. *Bond indenture*: A bond contract
43. *Bond issue costs*: Costs incurred in issuing bonds, such as legal and accounting fees and printing costs
44. *Bond retirement*: When a bond is paid and the liability is removed from the company’s balance sheet
45. *Bond sinking fund investment*: A fund established to accumulate assets to pay off bonds when they mature
46. *Bonding*: The process by which employees are investigated by an insurance company that will insure the business against losses through employee theft or mishandling of funds
47. *Bonds payable*: Long-term debt instruments that are written promises to repay the principal at a future date; interest is due at a fixed rate payable over the life of the bond
48. *Book value*: That portion of an asset’s original cost that has not yet been depreciated
49. *Book value (stock)*: The total equity applicable to a class of stock divided by the number of shares outstanding
50. *Brand name*: *See* Trade name
51. *Break even*: A point at which revenue equals expenses
52. *Break-even point (BEP)*: The sales volume when total revenue equals total expenses
53. *Budget*: An operating plan expressed in monetary units
54. *Budget performance report*: A comparison of actual costs and budgeted costs
55. *Business transaction*: A financial event that changes the resources of a firm
56. *Bylaws*: The guidelines for conducting a corporation’s business affairs
57. *Call price*: The amount the corporation must pay for the bond when it is called
58. *Callable bonds*: Bonds that allow the issuing corporation to require the holder to surrender the bonds for payment before their maturity date
59. *Callable preferred stock*: Stock that gives the issuing corporation the right to repurchase the preferred shares from the stockholders at a specific price
60. *Canceled check*: A check paid by the bank on which it was drawn
61. *Capacity*: A facility’s ability to produce or use
62. *Capital*: Financial investment in a business; equity
63. *Capital stock ledger*: A subsidiary ledger that contains a record of each stockholder’s purchases, transfers, and current balance of shares owned; also called stockholders’ ledger
64. *Capital stock transfer journal*: A record of stock transfers used for posting to the stockholders’ ledger
65. *Capitalized costs*: All costs recorded as part of an asset’s costs
66. *Carrying value of bonds*: The balance of the **Bonds Payable** account plus the **Premium on Bonds Payable** account minus the **Discount on Bonds Payable** account; also called *book value of bonds*
67. *Cash*: In accounting, currency, coins, checks, money orders, and funds on deposit in a bank
68. *Cash discount*: A discount offered by suppliers for payment received within a specified period of time
69. *Cash equivalents*: Assets that are easily convertible into known amounts of cash
70. *Cash payments journal*: A special journal used to record transactions involving the payment of cash
71. *Cash receipts journal*: A special journal used to record and post transactions involving the receipt of cash
72. *Cash register proof*: A verification that the amount of currency and coins in a cash register agrees with the amount shown on the cash register audit tape
Cash Short or Over account An account used to record any discrepancies between the amount of currency and coins in the cash register and the amount shown on the audit tape
1.  *Cashier’s check*: A draft on the issuing bank’s own funds
2.  *Certified Bookkeeper (CB)*: A designation that assures an individual possesses the level of knowledge and skill needed to carry out all key functions through the adjusted trial balance, including payroll
3.  *Certified public accountant (CPA)*: An independent accountant who provides accounting services to the public for a fee
4.  *Charge-account sales*: Sales made through the use of open-account credit or one of various types of credit cards
5.  *Chart of accounts*: A list of the accounts used by a business to record its financial transactions
6.  *Check*: A written order signed by an authorized person instructing a bank to pay a specific sum of money to a designated person or business
7.  *Chronological order*: Organized in the order in which the events occur
8.  *Classification*: A means of identifying each account as an asset, liability, or owner’s equity
9.  *Classified financial statement*: A format by which revenues and expenses on the income statement, and assets and liabilities on the balance sheet, are divided into groups of similar accounts and a subtotal is given for each group
10. *Closing entries*: Journal entries that transfer the results of operations (net income or net loss) to owner’s equity and reduce the revenue, expense, and drawing account balances to zero
11. *Collateral trust bonds*: Bonds secured by the pledge of securities, such as stocks or bonds of other companies
12. *Combined journal*: A journal that combines features of the general journal and the special journals in a single record
13. *Commercial draft*: A note issued by one party that orders another party to pay a specified sum on a specified date
14. *Commission basis*: A method of paying employees according to a percentage of net sales
15. *Common costs*: Costs not directly traceable to a specific segment of a business
16. *Common stock*: The general class of stock issued when no other class of stock is authorized; each share carries the same rights and privileges as every other share. Even if preferred stock is issued, common stock will also be issued Common Stock Dividend Distributable account  Equity account used to record par, or stated, value of shares to be issued as the result of the declaration of a stock dividend
1.  *Common-size statements*: Financial statements with items expressed as percentages of a base amount
2.  *Comparative statements*: Financial statements presented side by side for two or more years
3.  *Compensation record*: *See* Individual earnings record
4.  *Compound entry*: A journal entry with more than one debit or credit
5.  *Computer software*: An intangible asset; written programs that instruct a computer’s hardware to do certain tasks 
6.  *Conceptual framework*: A basic framework developed by the FASB to provide conceptual guidelines for financial statements. The most important features are statements of qualitative features of statements, basic assumptions underlying statements, basic accounting principles, and modifying constraints
7.  *Condensed income statement*: An income statement summarizing detailed income statement accounts into a few lines of information
8.  *Conservatism*: The concept that revenue and assets should be understated rather than overstated if GAAP allows alternatives. Similarly, expenses and liabilities should be overstated rather than understated
9.  *Contingent liability*: An item that can become a liability if certain things happen
10. *Contra account*: An account with a normal balance that is opposite that of a related account
11. *Contra asset account*: An asset account with a credit balance, which is contrary to the normal balance of an asset account
12. *Contra revenue account*: An account with a debit balance, which is contrary to the normal balance for a revenue account
13. *Contribution margin*: Gross profit on sales minus direct expenses; revenues minus variable costs
14. *Contribution margin*: Gross profit on sales minus direct expenses
15. *Control account*: An account that links a subsidiary ledger and the general ledger because its balance summarizes the balances of the accounts in the subsidiary ledger
16. *Controllable fixed costs*: Costs that the segment manager can control
17. *Convertible bonds*: Bonds that give the owner the right to convert the bonds into common stock under specified conditions
18. *Convertible preferred stock*: Preferred stock that conveys the right to convert that stock to common stock after a specified date or during a period of time
19. *Copyright*: An intangible asset; an exclusive right granted by the federal government to produce, publish, and sell a literary or artistic work for a period equal to the creator’s life plus 70 years
20. *Corporate charter*: A document issued by a state government that establishes a corporation
21. *Corporation*: A publicly or privately owned business entity that is separate from its owners and has a legal right to own property and do business in its own name; stockholders are not responsible for the debts or taxes of the business
22. *Correcting entry*: A journal entry made to correct an erroneous entry
23. *Cost center*: A business segment that incurs costs but does not produce revenue
24. *Cost of goods sold*: The actual cost to the business of the merchandise sold to customers
25. *Cost of production report*: Summarizes all costs charged to each department and shows the costs assigned to the goods transferred out of the department and to the goods still in process
26. *Cost variance*: The difference between the total standard cost and the total actual cost
27. *Cost-benefit test*: If accounting concepts suggest a particular accounting treatment for an item, but it appears that the theoretically correct treatment would require an unreasonable amount of work, the accountant may analyze the benefits and costs of the preferred treatment to see if the benefit gained from its adoption is justified by the cost
28. *Coupon bonds*: Unregistered bonds that have coupons attached for each interest payment; also called *bearer bonds*
29. *Credit*: An entry on the right side of an account
30. *Credit memorandum (accounts receivable)*: A note verifying that a customer’s account is being reduced by the amount of a sales return or sales allowance plus any sales tax that may have been involved
31. *Credit memorandum (banking)*: A form that explains any addition, other than a deposit, to a checking account, Terms for payment on credit by buyer to seller
1.  *Creditor*: One to whom money is owed
2.  *Cumulative preferred stock*: Stock that conveys to its owners the right to receive the preference dividend for the current year and any prior years in which the preference dividend was not paid before common stockholders receive any dividends
3.  *Current assets*: Assets consisting of cash, items that normally will be converted into cash within one year, and items that will be used up within one year
4.  *Current liabilities*: Debts that must be paid or otherwise satisfied within one year
5.  *Current ratio*: A relationship between current assets and current liabilities that provides a measure of a firm’s ability to pay its current debts (current ratio = current assets ÷ current liabilities)
6.  *Debentures*: Unsecured bonds backed only by a corporation’s general credit
7.  *Debit*: An entry on the left side of an account
8.  *Debit memorandum*: A form that explains any deduction, other than a check or other electronic payment transaction, from a checking account
9.  *Declaration date*: The date on which the board of directors declares a dividend
10. *Declining-balance method*: An accelerated method of depreciation in which an asset’s book value at the beginning of a year is multiplied by a constant percentage (such as 125%, 150%, or 200%) to determine depreciation for the year
11. *Deferred expenses*: *See* Prepaid expenses
12. *Deferred income*: *See* Unearned income
13. *Deferred income taxes*: The amount of income taxes that will be payable or an amount that may benefit the corporation in the future as a result of the difference between the current year’s taxable income and financial statement net income
14. *Departmental income statement*: Income statement that shows each department’s contribution margin and net income from operations after all expenses are allocated
15. *Depletion*: Allocating the cost of a natural resource to expense over the period in which the resource produces revenue
16. *Deposit in transit*: A deposit that is recorded in the cash receipts journal but that reaches the bank too late to be shown on the monthly bank statement
17. *Deposit slip*: A form prepared to record the deposit of cash or checks to a bank account
18. *Depreciation*: Allocation of the cost of a long-term asset to operations during its expected useful life
19. *Differential cost*: The difference in cost between one alternative and another 
20. *Direct charge-off method*: A method of recording uncollectible account losses as they occur
21. *Direct costing*: The accounting procedure whereby only variable costs are included in the cost of goods manufactured, and fixed manufacturing costs are written off as expenses in the period in which they are incurred
22. *Direct expenses*: Operating expenses that are identified directly with a department and are recorded by department
23. *Direct labor*: The costs attributable to personnel who work directly on the product being manufactured
24. *Direct materials*: All items that go into a product and become a part of it
25. *Direct method*: A means of reporting sources and uses of cash under which all revenue and expenses reported on the income statement appear in the operating section of the statement of cash flows and show the cash received or paid out for each type of transaction
26. *Discount on bonds payable*: The excess of the face value over the price received by the corporation for a bond
27. *Discount on credit card sales*: A fee charged by the credit card companies for processing sales made with credit cards.
28. *Discounting*: Deducting the interest from the principal on a note payable or receivable in advance
29. *Discussion memorandum*: An explanation of a topic under consideration by the Financial Accounting Standards Board
30. *Dishonored (NSF) check*: A check returned to the depositor unpaid because of insufficient funds in the drawer’s account; also called an NSF check 
31. *Dissolution*: The legal term for termination of a partnership
32. *Distributive share*: The amount of net income or net loss allocated to each partner
33. *Dividends*: Distributions of the profits of a corporation to its shareholders
34. *Donated capital*: Capital resulting from the receipt of gifts by a corporation
35. *Double-declining-balance (DDB) method*: A method of depreciation that uses a rate equal to twice the straight-line rate and applies that rate to the book value of the asset at the beginning of the year
36. *Double-entry system*: An accounting system that involves recording the effects of each transaction as debits and credits
37. *Draft*: A written order that requires one party (a person or business) to pay a stated sum of money to another party
38. *Drawee*: The bank on which a check is written
39. *Drawer*: The person or firm issuing a check
40. *Drawing account*: A special type of owner’s equity account set up to record the owner’s withdrawal of cash from the business
41. *Economic entity*: A business or organization whose major purpose is to produce a profit for its owners
42. *Electronic funds transfer (EFT)*: An electronic transfer of money from one account to another
43. *Employee*: A person who is hired by and works under the control and direction of the employer
44. *Employee’s Withholding Certificate, Form W-4*: A form used to claim filing status and other information for the business to use in determining the federal income tax to be withheld
45. *Employer’s Annual Federal Unemployment Tax Return, Form 940*: Preprinted government form used by the employer to report unemployment taxes for the calendar year
46. *Employer’s Quarterly Federal Tax Return, Form 941*:  Preprinted government form used by the employer to report payroll tax information relating to social security, ­Medicare, and employee income tax withholding to the Internal Revenue Service
47. *Endorsement*: A written authorization that transfers ownership of a check
48. *Entity*: Anything having its own separate identity, such as an individual, a town, a university, or a business
49. *Equity*: An owner’s financial interest in a business
50. *Equivalent production*: The estimated number of units that could have been started and completed with the same effort and costs incurred in the department during the same time period
51. *Exempt employees*: Salaried employees who hold supervisory or managerial positions who are not subject to the maximum hour and overtime pay provisions of the Wage and Hour Law
52. *Expense*: An outflow of cash, use of other assets, or incurring of a liability
53. *Experience rating system*: A system that rewards an employer for maintaining steady employment conditions by reducing the firm’s state unemployment tax rate
54. *Exposure draft*: A proposed solution to a problem being considered by the Financial Accounting Standards Board
55. *Face interest rate*: The contractual interest rate specified on the bond
56. *Face value*: An amount of money indicated to be paid, exclusive of interest or discounts
57. *Fair market value*: The current worth of an asset or the price the asset would bring if sold on the open market
58. *Federal unemployment taxes (FUTA)*: Taxes levied by the federal government against employers to benefit unemployed workers
59. *Financial statements*: Periodic reports of a firm’s financial position or operating results
60. *Financing activities*: Transactions with those who provide cash to the business to carry on its activities
61. *Finished goods inventory*: The cost of completed products ready for sale; corresponds to the  Merchandise Inventory  account of a merchandising business
62. *Finished goods subsidiary ledger*: A ledger containing a record for each of the different types of finished products
63. *First-in, first-out (FIFO) method*: A method of inventory costing that assumes the oldest merchandise is sold first
64. *Fixed budget*: A budget representing only one level of activity
65. *Fixed costs*: Costs that do not change in total as the level of ­activity changes
66. *Flexible budget*: A budget that shows the budgeted costs at ­various levels of activity
67. *Footing*: A small pencil figure written at the base of an amount column showing the sum of the entries in the column
68. *Franchise*: An intangible asset; a right to exclusive dealership granted by a governmental unit or a business entity
69. *Fraud*: Intentional or reckless acts that result in confiscation of a firm’s assets or the misrepresentation of the firm’s accounting data

Freight In account  An account showing transportation charges for items purchased 
1.  *Full disclosure principle*: The requirement that all information that might affect the user’s interpretation of the profitability and financial condition of a business be disclosed in the financial statements or in notes to the statements
2.  *Full endorsement*: A signature transferring a check to a specific person, firm, or bank
3.  *Fundamental accounting equation*: The relationship between assets and liabilities plus owner’s equity
4.  *Gain*: The disposition of an asset for more than its book value
5.  *General journal*: A financial record for entering all types of business transactions; a record of original entry
6.  *General ledger*: A permanent, classified record of all accounts used in a firm’s operation; a record of final entry
7.  *General partner*: A member of a partnership who has unlimited liability
8.  *Generally accepted accounting principles (GAAP)*: Accounting standards developed and applied by professional accountants
9.  *Going concern assumption*: The assumption that a firm will continue to operate indefinitely
10. *Goodwill*: An intangible asset; the value of a business in excess of the net value of its identifiable assets
11. *Governmental accounting*: Accounting work performed for a federal, state, or local governmental unit
12. *Gross profit*: The difference between net sales and the cost of goods sold (gross profit = net sales – cost of goods sold)
13. *Gross profit method*: A method of estimating inventory cost based on the assumption that the rate of gross profit on sales and the ratio of cost of goods sold to net sales are relatively constant from period to period
14. *Gross profit percentage*: The amount of gross profit from each dollar of sales (gross profit percentage = gross profit ÷ net sales)
15. *High-low point method*: A method to determine the fixed and variable components of a semivariable cost
16. *Historical cost basis principle*: The principle that requires assets and services to be recorded at their cost at the time they are acquired and that, generally, long-term assets remain at historical costs in the asset accounts
17. *Horizontal analysis*: Computing the percentage change for individual items in the financial statements from year to year
18. *Hourly rate basis*: A method of paying employees according to a stated rate per hour
19. *Impairment*: A situation that occurs when the asset is determined to have a fair market value less than its book value
20. *Income statement*: A formal report of business operations covering a specific period of time; also called a profit and loss statement or a statement of income and expenses 

Income Summary  A special owner’s equity account that is used only in the closing process to summarize the results of operations
1.  *Income tax method*: A method of recording the trade-in of an asset for income tax purposes. It does not permit a gain or loss to be recognized on the transaction
2.  *Independent contractor*: One who is paid by a company to carry out a specific task or job but is not under the direct supervision or control of the company
3.  *Indirect expenses*: Operating expenses that cannot be readily identified and are not closely related to activity within a department
4.  *Indirect labor*: Costs attributable to personnel who support production but are not directly involved in the manufacture of a product; for example, supervisory, repair and maintenance, and janitorial staff
5.  *Indirect materials and supplies*: Materials used in manufacturing a product that may not become a part of the product and are not a significant material cost factor
6.  *Indirect method*: A means of reporting cash generated from operating activities by treating net income as the primary source of cash in the operating section of the statement of cash flows and adjusting that amount for changes in current assets and liabilities associated with net income, noncash transactions, and other items
7.  *Individual earnings record*: An employee record that contains information needed to compute earnings and complete tax reports
8.  *Industry averages*: Financial ratios and percentages reflecting averages for similar companies
9.  *Industry practice constraint*: In a few limited cases, unusual operating characteristics of an industry, usually based on risk, for which special accounting principles and procedures have been developed. These may not conform completely with GAAP for other industries
10. *Intangible assets*: Assets that lack a physical substance, such as goodwill, patents, copyrights, and computer software, although software has, in a sense, a physical attribute
11. *Interest*: The fee charged for the use of money
12. *Internal control*: A company’s policies and procedures to safeguard assets, ensure reliability of accounting data, and promote compliance with management policies and applicable laws
13. *International accounting*: The study of accounting principles used by different countries
14. *Interpret*: To understand and explain the meaning and importance of something (such as financial statements)
15. *Inventory sheet*: A form used to list the quantity and type of goods a firm has in stock
16. *Inventory turnover*: The number of times inventory is purchased and sold during the accounting period (inventory turnover = cost of goods sold ÷ average inventory)
17. *Investing activities*: Transactions that involve the acquisition or disposal of long-term assets
18. *Invoice*: A customer billing for merchandise bought on credit
19. *Job order*: A specific order for a specific batch of manufactured items
20. *Job order cost accounting*: A cost accounting system that determines the unit cost of manufactured items for each separate production order
21. *Job order cost sheet*: A record of all manufacturing costs charged to a specific job
22. *Journal*: The record of original entry
23. *Journalizing*: Recording transactions in a journal
24. *Just-in-time system*: An inventory system in which raw materials are ordered so they arrive just in time to be placed into production
25. *Labor efficiency variance*: *See * Labor time variance
26. *Labor rate variance*: The difference between the actual labor rate per hour and the standard labor rate per hour multiplied by the actual number of hours worked on the job
27. *Labor time variance*: The difference between the actual hours worked and the standard labor hours allowed for the job multiplied by the standard cost per hour
28. *Last-in, first-out (LIFO) method*: A method of inventory costing that assumes that the most recently purchased merchandise is sold first
29. *Ledger*: The record of final entry
30. *Leveraged buyout*: Purchasing a business by acquiring the stock and obligating the business to pay the debt incurred
31. *Leveraging*: Using borrowed funds to earn a profit greater than the interest that must be paid on the borrowing
32. *Liabilities*: Debts or obligations of a business
33. *Limited liability company (LLC)*: Provides limited liability to the owners, who can elect to have the profits taxed at the LLC level or on their individual tax returns
34. *Limited liability partnership (LLP)*: A partnership that provides some limited liability for all partners
35. *Limited partner*: A member of a partnership whose liability is limited to his or her investment in the partnership
36. *Limited partnership*: A partnership having one or more limited partners
37. *Liquidation*: Termination of a business by distributing all assets and discontinuing the business
38. *Liquidation value*: Value of assets to be applied to preferred stock, usually par value or an amount in excess of par value, if the corporation is liquidated
39. *Liquidity*: The ease with which an item can be converted into cash; the ability of a business to pay its debts when due
40. *List price*: An established retail price
41. *Long-term liabilities*: Debts of a business that are due more than one year in the future
42. *Loss*: The disposition of an asset for less than its book value
43. *Lower of cost or net realizable value rule*: The principle by which inventory is reported at either its original cost or its net realizable value, whichever is lower
44. *Management advisory services*: Services designed to help clients improve their information systems or their business performance
45. *Managerial accounting*: Accounting work carried on by an accountant employed by a single business in industry; the branch of accounting that provides financial information about business segments, activities, or products
46. *Manufacturing business*: A business that sells goods that it has produced
47. *Manufacturing cost budget*: A budget made for each manufacturing cost
48. *Manufacturing margin*: Sales minus the variable cost of  goods sold
49. *Manufacturing overhead*: All manufacturing costs that are not classified as direct materials or direct labor
50. *Manufacturing overhead ledger*: A subsidiary ledger that contains a record for each overhead item

Manufacturing Summary account  The account to which all items on the statement of cost of goods manufactured are closed; similar to the  Income Summary  account
1.  *Marginal income*: The manufacturing margin minus variable operating expenses
2.  *Markdown*: Price reduction below the original markon
3.  *Market interest rate*: The interest rate a corporation is willing to pay and investors are willing to accept at the current time
4.  *Market value*: The price per share at which stock is bought and sold
5.  *Markon*: The difference between the cost and the initial retail price of merchandise
6.  *Markup*: A price increase above the original markon
7.  *Matching principle*: The concept that revenue and the costs incurred in earning the revenue should be matched in the appropriate accounting periods
8.  *Materiality constraint*: The significance of an item in relation to a particular situation or set of facts
9.  *Materials price variance*: The difference between the actual price and the standard cost for materials multiplied by the actual quantity of materials used
10. *Materials quantity variance*: The difference between the actual quantity used and the quantity of materials allowed multiplied by the standard cost of the materials
11. *Materials requisition*: A form that describes the item and quantity needed and shows the job or purpose
12. *Materials usage variance*: See Materials quantity variance
13. *Maturity value*: The total amount (principal plus interest) payable when a note comes due
14. *Medicare tax*: A tax levied on employees and employers to provide medical care for the employee and the employee’s spouse after each has reached age 65
15. *Memorandum entry*: An informational entry in the general journal
16. *Merchandise inventory*: The stock of goods a merchandising business keeps on hand
17. *Merchandising business*: A business that sells goods purchased for resale
18. *Merit rating system*: *See* Experience rating system
19. *Minute book*: A book in which accurate and complete records of all meetings of stockholders and directors are kept
20. *Monetary unit assumption*: It is assumed that only those items and events that can be measured in monetary terms are included in the financial statements. An inherent part of this assumption is that the monetary unit is stable. Thus, assets purchased one year may be combined in the accounts with those purchased in other years even though the dollars used in each year actually may have different purchasing power
21. *Mortgage loan*: A long-term debt created when a note is given as part of the purchase price for land or buildings
22. *Multiple-step income statement*: A type of income statement on which several subtotals are computed before the net income is calculated
23. *Mutual agency*: The characteristic of a partnership by which each partner is empowered to act as an agent for the partnership, binding the firm by their acts
24. *Negotiable*: A financial instrument whose ownership can be transferred to another person or business
25. *Negotiable instrument*: A financial document containing a promise or order to pay that meets all requirements of the Uniform Commercial Code in order to be transferable to another party
26. *Net book value*: The cost of an asset minus its accumulated depreciation, depletion, or amortization; also known as book value
27. *Net income*: The result of an excess of revenue over expenses
